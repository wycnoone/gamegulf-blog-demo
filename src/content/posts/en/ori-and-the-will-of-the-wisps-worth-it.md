## What kind of game is Ori and the Will of the Wisps, really?

**Ori and the Will of the Wisps is a movement-first Metroidvania that mixes elegant platforming with more combat and boss structure than the first game.** It is not just "pretty Ori again" — it is a broader and more confident sequel.

1. **Traversal is the headline feature.** Dash, grapple, bash, burrow, and chained movement upgrades make the game feel incredible in your hands once the toolkit opens up.
2. **Combat matters more this time.** Spirit weapons and shards give fights more texture, which helps the sequel feel less passive than Ori and the Blind Forest.
3. **The campaign stays tight.** HowLongToBeat puts the game around 12 hours for the main path and 16-18 hours for fuller runs, which is long enough to feel substantial without turning bloated.

If you like Hollow Knight, Metroid Dread, or the sharper side of action-platformers, Ori is an easy fit. If you want zero backtracking and low-stress platforming, it will feel more demanding than its art style suggests.

## How does Ori and the Will of the Wisps run on Switch?

**Ori and the Will of the Wisps is widely considered one of the more impressive Switch ports, with a 60 fps target and strong overall image quality for the hardware.** The version on Nintendo Switch supports TV, tabletop, and handheld play, and the feel of movement survives the port extremely well.

Here is the practical takeaway for Switch players:

- **Frame rate:** generally targets 60 fps, which is crucial for a game built around dash timing and chained movement
- **Image quality:** dynamic resolution scaling keeps the presentation sharp enough without sacrificing responsiveness
- **Handheld experience:** especially strong because the game works well in shorter sessions and still looks striking on the smaller screen
- **Content parity:** this is the full single-player game, not a cut-down version
